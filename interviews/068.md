Interviewer: I'd like to start with the last time you decided what to watch. Can you walk me through that experience?

P: Oh god, yeah. So it was probably Tuesday night. My husband was working late, kids were in bed, and I had maybe 30 minutes before I passed out. I just... I opened Netflix first, scrolled for like five minutes, closed it. Then opened Disney+ because I remembered seeing something about a new season of something there. Then I think I opened Apple TV? I don't even remember what I was looking for at that point. I ended up watching 15 minutes of some cooking show I've seen three times already.

Interviewer: Why'd you bounce around like that instead of just picking one?

P: Because I couldn't remember where I was watching things, honestly. My brain was fried. I knew there was something I wanted to see, but I had to remember which subscription it was on. And half the time I guess wrong anyway. Like, I'll think something's on Netflix and it's actually on Hulu.

Interviewer: Does this happen a lot?

P: Constantly. And the worse part is my ten-year-old will ask me "can we watch The Bear?" and I have no idea if we have it or if we need to subscribe to something new. So I'm like "maybe, let me check," and then I'm clicking around on three different apps. Takes forever.

Interviewer: What about managing the subscriptions themselves? How do you handle that?

P: I don't, really. My husband technically pays for some of them. I know we have Netflix, Disney+, his work gives him a Hulu account... there's probably others. We got a free month of something last year and I genuinely don't know if we're still paying for it. I should probably check but it feels like a nightmare to untangle. Like, one of them might be coming out of my credit card and I won't even know.

Interviewer: So you don't have a good system.

P: No system at all. It's just chaos. I spend money, he spends money, stuff piles up. I know there's overlap too—like, we definitely have two ways to watch the same things. It's wasteful.

Interviewer: How does that make you feel?

P: Frustrated, honestly. Like, I'm paying for something and not using it. And also guilty—like, I should be more on top of it, but I have a second grader and a fifth grader and work, so by the time I sit down, I want to just hit play, not solve a jigsaw puzzle about which app has what.

Interviewer: Tell me about a time recently when you actually did watch something with someone else—not alone.

P: Okay, so my college roommate lives in Denver. We were trying to watch something together last month. We both had the same subscription—we thought—and I spent like 20 minutes on FaceTime with her trying to sync up a show, and then she realized her mom had a different sign-in on her account or something. So we just... gave up? I recommended something instead and we said we'd watch it separately and talk about it later. Which is fine, but it felt dumb. Like, we could have been watching it at the same time, commenting on it, and instead we're just going to text about it after. It's not the same.

Interviewer: Has that happened with other people?

P: My sister lives two hours away. We wanted to watch something together on a Sunday afternoon—she was coming over. So we tried to find something we both had access to, and it was such a pain that we literally just turned on a YouTube video instead. Which sounds silly but that's what happened. The friction was so high that we just gave up on the streaming services entirely.

Interviewer: What would have made that easier?

P: I don't know. Just... knowing what we both had? Or having a way to share it? I don't even know if that's a thing. I just remember thinking, "Why is this so hard?" We were trying to have a nice time and we ended up watching someone's mukbang instead.

Interviewer: Let me ask about the actual act of deciding—when you do pick something. What goes into that decision?

P: Usually I'm looking for something I don't have to think about too hard. Like, I'm not looking for the next great drama. I'm looking for something that if I miss five minutes because I'm answering a text or the kids come back downstairs, I'm not lost. So a lot of reality TV, rewatches, cooking shows. Stuff I can half-watch.

Interviewer: Do recommendations matter to you?

P: Yeah, but I don't trust the algorithm anymore. Netflix is like, "Because you watched this," and I'm like, "That has nothing to do with what I actually want." I usually go off what my friends say. But I have to remember to ask them, or wait for someone to bring something up in a group chat.

Interviewer: Do you ever feel like you're missing something good?

P: All the time. My friends are always talking about shows and I'm like, "Oh, I never heard of that. Is it on... something?" And then sometimes they tell me what they're watching and I already have that service and I had no idea the show was even there. So yes, I definitely feel like I'm missing stuff.

Interviewer: How much do you think about the cost side of things?

P: More since inflation. We're probably paying $80, $90 a month across everything and I genuinely have no idea if we're using it all. I know we watch Netflix and Disney+ regularly. Hulu, maybe? And there's something else I'm forgetting. It adds up and every month I'm like, "We should probably cut one," and then we never do because I don't know which one to cut.

Interviewer: Last thing—if you could change one thing about how you watch TV, what would it be?

P: Just, like, one place where I could see everything. Not that I want to pay one company for everything, but just... I want to know what I have and what's on it without opening six apps. That's it.
