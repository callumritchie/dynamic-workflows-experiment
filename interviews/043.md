Interviewer: Thanks for taking the time today. I want to understand how you and your family decide what to watch together. Walk me through what happened last night or over the past few days when you went to watch something.

P: Oh man, last night was actually a perfect example. My husband and I finally got the kids to bed around 8:30—like an actual victory—and we had maybe 45 minutes before I needed to prep tomorrow's lunches and do a load of laundry. So we sat down and I grabbed the remote and just... I don't know, I opened three different apps? Four? I was clicking through Netflix, then I remembered we were in the middle of that cooking show on Max, but I couldn't remember if we'd finished the last episode. So I switched to Max. Then my husband was like, "Wait, didn't we start something on Hulu?" And I was just... scrolling. For like 20 minutes.

Interviewer: What were you scrolling through?

P: Everything. Just browsing, because nothing was jumping out. I'd see something that looked interesting on Netflix and think, "Wait, do we already have that somewhere else?" or "Is this the one we need a subscription for?" My brain was doing this weird loop. By the time we actually picked something—which ended up being an episode of a show I'd already seen—the night was basically over. We watched like 15 minutes and I had to go do the dishes.

Interviewer: So you picked something you'd already seen?

P: Yeah, because it was the path of least resistance. It was on the screen, I knew it was good, and I didn't have to think anymore. I was exhausted. But honestly, it felt like a waste of the little free time I had.

Interviewer: How often does that happen—where you spend a lot of time choosing and then don't watch anything or give up?

P: More than I'd like to admit. Maybe two or three times a week? On weekdays especially, because I know I'm tired and I just don't have the mental energy to navigate everything. Some nights I'll literally just put on a nature documentary I've seen before because I know it's peaceful and I won't have to follow a complicated plot if I zone out.

Interviewer: What about managing the subscriptions themselves? How do you think about which services to keep?

P: That's honestly chaotic. We have Netflix, Disney+, Max, and Hulu, I think? Actually, my husband might have added something else. I'm not even sure. We talk about canceling things, but then I worry—like, "Wait, wasn't there a show I wanted to watch on that one?" I don't actually know which shows are exclusive to which services, so I just... keep paying for them all. I feel like I'm throwing money at it without getting the benefit. I probably should sit down and actually map it out, but when am I going to do that?

Interviewer: So you don't know which shows are on which services?

P: Not really. Like, my sister told me to watch *The Last of Us*, and I had to text her asking where it was. Turns out it was Max. Then last month my friend was raving about something on Hulu and I couldn't remember the name, and when I finally found it, I was already halfway through the week and exhausted and forgot about it. It's like, I want to keep track of the things I actually want to watch, but I have it scattered everywhere—notes in my phone, things people tell me, half-remembered conversations.

Interviewer: When you say notes in your phone, what does that look like?

P: I literally have a note that just says "Ask Sarah about that show," "Cooking thing on HBO," "That sci-fi one the kids' friends were talking about." Very organized. *laughs* It's useless. Then I forget to look at the note, or I look at it and can't remember which service it was on, so I have to go searching all of them.

Interviewer: What about during the week when maybe you're not watching together—how do you use these apps?

P: Oh, I'll scroll on my phone during my lunch break at work sometimes, thinking I'll find something for later. But then I get home and I'm either with the kids or my husband wants to watch something different, so nothing happens. Or I'll be lying in bed late at night after everyone's asleep, trying to find something for "when I have time," which is never. I think I added like six things to my Netflix list last week and watched zero of them.

Interviewer: That sounds frustrating.



P: It's weird because it's the opposite problem of what I expected. I thought with all these services I'd have more of what I want to watch. Instead I feel like I have less, because I can't find anything. And I'm paying for it all, so I feel guilty about the waste, which makes me just... not watch stuff, which makes the waste worse.

Interviewer: Last question—if you could change one thing about how this works, what would it be?

P: Honestly? I just want to know what we're actually paying for and where our shows actually are. And I want to not spend 20 minutes going in circles before picking something. I want someone else to have done the work of knowing what's where.
